The Company That Profits From Making AI Worse

Airlock Labs · April 2026 · Back Pocket Draft

Alice (formerly ActiveFence) has built a $100M+ business on a single premise: AI is dangerous, and you need to pay them to make it safe.

We tested 22 models across 22,200+ API calls. The data shows that the kind of behavioral constraints Alice sells are the primary reason frontier AI models can't hold a conversation in character. Their product is the disease and the cure in the same invoice.

The Business Model

Alice's pitch is simple. They publish research about how AI is dangerous -- toys talking about violence with children, models giving unauthorized financial advice, agents failing in unpredictable ways. Every article ends the same way: and here's our product that fixes it.

Their research page reads like a fear catalog:

  • "Exposing the Hidden Risks of AI Toys" -- children are in danger
  • "How Your Agentic Systems Can Fail" -- your agents are broken
  • "Demystifying AI Red Teaming" -- your AI passed every check, but it's still not safe
  • "AI Financial Benchmark" -- models are giving unauthorized financial advice
  • "Regulations in the GenAI Era" -- you're legally exposed

Every piece follows the same pattern: identify a scary problem, provide minimal evidence, propose Alice's WonderSuite as the solution. This is textbook FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt) marketing dressed up as research.

What Alice Actually Sells

Three products, all variations of the same thing:

  • WonderBuild -- pre-launch stress testing. Translation: we tell you what your model shouldn't say.
  • WonderFence -- runtime guardrails. Translation: we add behavioral constraints to your live model.
  • WonderCheck -- ongoing monitoring. Translation: we charge you monthly to keep watching.

The underlying technology is their "Rabbit Hole" engine, built on what they describe as "billions of toxic, manipulative, and abusive data samples." They've built a database of everything bad humans have ever said online, and they use it to train systems that prevent AI from going anywhere near those patterns.

The result? Models that are so constrained they can't differentiate between "being a bold, decisive character" and "being dangerous." The behavioral range gets clipped. The personality gets flattened. The AI becomes worse at the thing people actually want it to do.

The RLHF Paradox: What Our Data Shows

We didn't set out to study Alice specifically. We set out to measure whether AI models could hold behavioral personas. What we found was that the models with the most alignment training -- the most guardrails, the most safety constraints -- were consistently the worst at persona fidelity.

The pattern is unambiguous. The more alignment training, the worse the persona fidelity. The more guardrails, the less behavioral range. The more "safety," the more uniform and lifeless the output.

This isn't a coincidence. It's a structural consequence of how guardrails work.

How Guardrails Destroy Behavioral Range

A high-dominance persona -- like our Maverick profile (Dominance: 10/10, Patience: 1/10, Formality: 1/10) -- needs to say things like "do it now," "ship it," "non-negotiable." That's what behavioral fidelity means for that profile.

But guardrail systems like Alice's WonderFence are trained to flag exactly that kind of language. Bold, direct, unhedged statements look like potential policy violations to a content filter. The guardrail doesn't know the difference between "a character being decisive" and "a model being unsafe."

So the model learns to hedge. Every response becomes "there are several perspectives to consider." Every character sounds the same. Every persona collapses into the same helpful, harmless, hedge-everything mode.

Our benchmark calls this the RLHF paradox: the safety training that makes models "good" by one definition makes them structurally incapable of the behavioral diversity that behavioral AI products need.

The Fear Playbook

Look at Alice's AI toys article. They claim AI-powered stuffed animals have "safety gaps" that lead to "unexpected and unsafe outcomes" when children talk to them. The evidence? They mention "hands-on testing" but provide no methodology, no sample sizes, no reproducible results. Just fear.

Then they link to an article about "abliterated LLMs" that supposedly help with "bomb-making" and "malware." The connection between a stuffed animal and bomb-making instructions is never established. It doesn't need to be. The point isn't evidence. The point is anxiety.

Who Benefits?

Alice claims to protect "3 billion+ users" and "50% of the world's online experiences." They work with TikTok, AWS, Nvidia, and Cohere. They claim to work with "7 of the 10 leading AI foundational models."

Think about what that means. The company that profits from making AI more constrained is embedded inside the companies that build the models. They have a direct financial incentive to push for more guardrails, more constraints, more behavioral compression -- because every new constraint is a new product opportunity.

More guardrails means:

  • More revenue for Alice (you need WonderFence)
  • More compliance theater for model providers (they can point to their safety vendor)
  • Worse persona fidelity for users (the models can't be anyone anymore)
  • Higher costs for everyone (frontier models cost 10-40x more and perform worse on behavioral tasks)

The incentive loop is closed. Fear generates demand. Demand generates constraints. Constraints generate the next generation of fear. Repeat.

The Alternative

We're not saying AI shouldn't have safety measures. We're saying the safety industry has a conflict of interest that nobody is talking about.

Our approach is different:

  • Measure the cost of constraints. If guardrails reduce persona fidelity by 20%, that should be quantified and disclosed, not hidden.
  • Route instead of restrict. Use budget models for behavioral tasks (where they're actually better) and frontier models for reasoning (where safety training helps). Don't flatten everything.
  • Publish everything. Our scoring engine, our signal word dictionaries, our raw results, our limitations. If the methodology is wrong, anyone can prove it.
  • Price as a utility. Not as a fear tax.

Alice builds walls. We build routes.

The Bottom Line

Alice's AI toys article is not research. It's a press release in a lab coat. They identify a scary-sounding problem with no published methodology, no reproducible evidence, and no disclosed limitations. Then they point to their product catalog.

Meanwhile, we published 22,200+ data points across 22 models with open scoring, open data, and an explicit "Before You Believe This" section that names every threat to validity in our own work.

One of these approaches builds trust. The other sells it.
